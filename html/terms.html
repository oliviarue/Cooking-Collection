Food Safety & Preparation

  1. Parboiling: Partially boiling food to soften them before further cooking.
  2. Sanitizing: Using heat or chemicals to kill bacteria to prevent contamination.
  3. Cross-Contamination: The transfer of bacteria from one food or surface to another.
  4. Blanching: Quickly boiling fruits or vegetables, followed by immediate submersion in ice water.
  5. Cold Smoking: Flavoring and preserving food by exposing it to smoke at low temperatures without fully cooking it.

Baking

  1. Blind Baking: Pre-baking a pie crust without filling, often using weights to prevent bubbling.
  2. Docking: Prickling dough with a fork to allow steam to escape durin gbaking, preventing puffing.
  3. Retarding: Slowing down yeast activity by refrigeration, allowing more time for flavor development.
  4. Aytolyse: The resting period where flour and water and mixed before kneading, allowing gluten development.
  5. Scoring: Making shallow cuts on the surface of dough before baking to control expansion and create patterns.

Flavoring and Seasoning

  1. Infusion: Steeping herbs, spices, or other flavorings in a liquid to extract their flavors.
  2. Roux: A mixture of fat and flour used as a thickening agent for soups, stews, and sauces.
  3. Rubbing: Massaging a blend of spices, salt, and herbs into meat or poultry before cooking.
  4. Sweating: Gently cooking vegetables in fat to soften them without browning to release natural flavors.
  5. Reduction: Cooking a liquid over low heat to evaporate water and intensify flavors, often used for sauces.

Meat and Seafood

  1. Trimming: Removing excess fat or sinew from meat to improve texture and presentation.
  2. Dry Aging: Storing meat in a controlled environment to enhance flavor and tenderness over time.
  3. Butterflying: Cutting meat or seafood so that it opens like a book, allowing for even cooking or stuffing.
  4. Scalding: Briefly immersing poultry or seafood in hot water to loosen feathers or scales for easier removal.
  5. Silver Skin: The tough, connective tissue found on some cuts of meat that must be removed for better tenderness.

Temperature and Doneness

  1. Pinking: A slight pink color in cooked meat, indicating a specific level of doneness.
  2. Carryover Cooking: The process where food continues to cook after being removed from the heat source.
  3. Sous Vide Pasteurization: Cooking food in a water bath at a controlled temperature to eliminate bacteria.
  4. Soft Ball Stage: A stage in candy making where syrup dropped into cold water forms a soft, malleable ball.
  5. Hard Crack Stage: A stage in candy making where syrup dropped into cold water forms hard, brittle threads.

Plating and Serving

  1. Napping: Coating food with a thin layer of sauce for added flavor and visual appeal.
  2. Chiffonade: Finely slicing leafy herbs or greens into thin ribbons for garnish or flavor.
  3. Sous Cloche: Presenting a dish under a dome cover, usually for dramatic effect when served.
  4. Coulis: A thick sauce made from pureed fruits or vegetables, often used for plating or garnishing.
  5. Canap√©: A decorative appetizer usually served on bread or crackers, often topped with spreads or garnishes.

Preservation

  1. Smoking: Using smoke to flavor and preserve food.
  2. Dehydrating: Removing moisture from food to preserve it and extend its shelf life.
  3. Canning: Preserving food by processing in sealed jars to prevent spoilage from bacteria.
  4. Jellying: Preserving food by cooking it with sugar and pectin to create a gel-like consistency.
  5. Clarifying: Removing impurities by heating and skimming off unwanted particles, resulting in a clear product.
